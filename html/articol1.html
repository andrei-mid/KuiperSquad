The magic behind flight

Have you ever wondered how airplanes fly? How do these incredible machines manage to lift off the ground?


In short, it’s not magic—it’s the result of over a century of innovation and remarkable engineering. Understanding how airplanes fly takes us back to the development of modern aerodynamics.


A Brief History: Bernoulli’s Principle


To understand how airplanes generate lift, let’s explore a key concept: Bernoulli’s Principle, introduced by Daniel Bernoulli in 1738. This principle explains that in an uninterrupted flow of fluid (or air), the total pressure along a streamline remains constant. Bernoulli's equation has been instrumental in understanding part of the lift mechanism.



Breaking Down Bernoulli’s Equation


P + ½ ρ v2 + ρ gh = constant


  • P: Fluid pressure
  • ρ: Fluid density
  • v: Fluid velocity
  • g: Acceleration due to gravity
  • h: Height above a reference level

Static Pressure:This is the pressure exerted by air (or any fluid) at rest. It can be thought of as the force of air molecules pushing against a surface. As the area containing the air increases, static pressure increases, and vice versa.



Dynamic Pressure:Represented by the term "½ρv²," dynamic pressure depends on the speed (velocity) and density of the airflow. As the speed of the air increases, dynamic pressure increases. This becomes important when we talk about how air moves around a wing.


While Bernoulli’s Principle helps explain the airflow over a wing, it’s only part of the story. To fully understand lift, we must also consider Newton’s Third Law of Motion.


Newton’s Third Law and Lift


Newton’s Third Law states that "for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction." When air flows over a wing, the shape and tilt of the wing (known as the angle of attack) deflect air downward. This creates an upward reaction force, contributing to lift.

Simply put, as the wing pushes air down, the air pushes the wing up. This is another important way lift is generated.


Applying Bernoulli’s and Newton’s Principles: How a Wing Works



To visualize these principles at work, let’s apply them to a wing (also known as an airfoil). The curvature of the wing's upper surface means that air traveling over the top must move faster than air traveling under the wing. According to Bernoulli’s Principle, faster airflow results in lower pressure, while the slower air below the wing maintains higher pressure. This pressure difference helps generate lift.

But that’s not all! The angle of attack—the angle at which the wing meets the oncoming air—also plays a crucial role. When a wing is angled upward, it deflects air downward. Newton’s Third Law explains that as the wing pushes air down, the reaction force pushes the wing (and the airplane) upward.


A Common Example: The Venturi Effect


The Venturi effect, which occurs in devices like carburetors, offers a helpful analogy. As air flows through a narrowing passage, it speeds up, and the pressure decreases—just like the airflow over the top of a wing. Although this example is not a perfect match for a wing, it helps illustrate the relationship between airflow speed and pressure.


The Wonders of Lift: A Balanced Explanation


The generation of lift is not based on just one theory but rather a combination of Bernoulli’s Principle, Newton’s Third Law, and airfoil design. The pressure difference above and below the wing, the deflection of air downward, and the angle of attack all work together to keep an airplane in the sky.

However, it’s important to note that lift is a complex phenomenon, and the full science is still being refined. There are additional concepts, like circulation theory, that also play a role in explaining lift.
